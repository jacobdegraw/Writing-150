ACES

Argument

The argument corresponds with logos. It encompasses the logic of what we are trying to say. It consists of three things: The claim, the reason, and the assumption.

  • Claim: The claim is the main point that we are trying to make with our writing.

  • Reason: The reason is the support that we give for our claim. A claim without a reason is not an argument.

  • Assumption: The assumption is the (usually) unspoken values, beliefs, and principles that the rhetor assumes they share with their audience. These assumptions are required to complete the argument.

Character

Character corresponds with ethos. It has everything to do with our credibility. The more we can build our credibility as rhetors, the stronger our writing becomes. Here are some ways to build our character in our writing:


  • Do your homework. You must make sure that you understand what you are talking about, meaning that you have done your research and are knowledgable about the subject.

  • Use personal experience. Use relevant, appropriate experiences to show that you have experiences with the subject. It must, however, be genuine and fit for the rhetorical situation.

  • Show balance. Show a balanced view by presenting and responding to counterclaims. This shows that you have considered both sides.

Emotion

Emotion corresponds with pathos. People are heavily influenced by their emotions, and so if you can learn how to invoke the right emotions for whatever you are trying to say, you can make your writing much more powerful. Here are some suggestions:


  • Tell stories. Storie have a way of engaging readers and making them emotionally invested in whatever you are saying.

  • Tap shared values. One of the best ways to evoke emotion is to tap into important values that you share with your readers.

  • Amplify with word choice. This kind of goes along with style, but choosing words that illicit an emotional response is a great way to change the feeling of the writing.

Style

Style has to do with the syntax, or the way that you actually write. There are four 'virtues' of style that we discussed in class.


  • Fitting: The style that we use must fit the rhetorical situation.

  • Clean: Our writin needs to be clean. We should avoid any kind of errors in our grammar, spelling, punctation, diction, etc.

  • Clear: Our writing must clearly convey the message that we are trying to send, otherwise the reader won't know what we are trying to say.

  • Compelling: We can follow the other virtues of style but still have a piece of writing that nobody wants to read. If we don't make it interesting to read, it won't get read.

Rhetorical Analysis

We can analyze our rhetorical situation using the GRAPE acronym. The better that we understand our rhetorical situation, the better we can tailor our writing to fit into what we want it to accomplish.


Genre
Genre is the medium that we are using to convey a rhetorical message, or the 'form' that it might take.

Rhetor
The speaker, writer, or creator of a message.

Audience
The person or group of people that a piece of writing is meant for.

Purpose
The reason that a piece of rhetoric was created.

Exigence
What triggered the rhetor to create the piece of rhetoric.

Writing Process

Plan

The first step in the writing process is planning. We start to think about the rhetorical situation and how we are going to approach it. We can start to research (depending on the genre) and make an outline. Everybody will do this a little bit differently but as long as you have something planned out you will be much more effective as you start to write.

Practice

The second step is practice. Practice is how it gets from planing to the finished product. Practice will look different for everybody. Maybe you write a draft, go back and edit it, maybe you revise your draft as you go. The most important thing is to find what works for you and go with it.


Revise

Revision is the next part of the process. When we revise, it is more than just checking for grammatical errors right before turning it in. We should be analyzing it to see where we can improve and make it more effective. Having peers go over our writing with us can help us to see places we can improve that we might not have seen on our own.


Reflect

After we finally finish our work of rhetoric, it is time to reflect. This is very important, because it is where we think about what went well, what went poorly, and how we can improve in the future. This is another place where metacognition comes in, because we can think about it not just in terms of the content, but in terms of our process. Then we can improve our process, which will greatly improve our writing.
